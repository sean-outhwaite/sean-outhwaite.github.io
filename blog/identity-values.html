About Me

My name is Sean, though most of my friends call me Swan (this started as a typo of my name but it stuck). After high school I wasn’t sure what I wanted to do, so instead of committing to study right away I started working in tech support for an ISP. Since then I have gone on to work various roles at companies such as Mighty Ape, Vend(now Lightspeed) and most recently I was a Product Manager at Fergus for four years.

My parents are great role models, growing up they instilled in me strong values for things like basic manners and treating all people with respect. Throughout my life, making friends and working with people from all sorts of different backgrounds has taught me a lot about being more aware of my own privilege and considerate of other people.

Ethics

I think almost every decision you make day to day has some ethical connotations but one big one for me was several years ago I decided to go vegan. Although I care about animal welfare I’m not opposed to eating meat/dairy as I think that’s a part of natural life, my main motivators were the environmental impact of the meat + dairy industry, and treatment of animals at these facilities. I read up about the impact these things have here in Aotearoa and how our waterways are polluted by the farming industry, I thought about my diet and the things I typically eat and how this change would affect me personally. I came to the conclusion that giving up these things wouldn’t be a huge challenge for me and that the pros would outweigh the cons.

I kept that up for about 5 years but eventually returned to an omni diet. I found it difficult being a bit of a burden on those around me (having to pick spots with vegan options, providing vegan food at social gatherings, etc). I am still proud of the decision to try it and to have stuck with it for so long, and even today it still impacts the choices I make. I eat less meat than I used to, try to make sure it’s ethically sourced, and have switched exclusively to oat milk (honestly it’s just better).

My Strengths

Across my career I’ve learned loads of different skills, from more technical things like IT skills and SQL, to soft skills like stakeholder management and communication (while I was at Vend I was even sent to a conference in Hong Kong to run workshops with one of our larger customers!). **Insert photo of Hong Kong with caption explaining phone died during the trip**

Having worked in Support for several years, I love problem solving. Coming across an issue, finding the root cause, and working towards a solution bring me a lot of satisfaction. I’ve found even in my later career as a PM, the part I enjoyed the most was when bugs were reported in my area and I was able to spend time replicating & diagnosing these issues.

Past coworkers have described me as a “rock” for my team, that I stay calm and collected in tough situations and help to ground others when we encounter unexpected changes.

Generally I’m a pretty quiet person, I used to get quite anxious about things like public speaking (I still do! But am better equipped to overcome it now). As I’ve grown I’ve tried to be more open minded about getting out of my comfort zone and challenging myself more (committing to dev academy was one of those challenges). Somewhat ironically though, I’ve been told I’m an excellent communicator. As a PM I was required to frequently present in front of the whole company or directly to execs, through practice and experience I’ve learned how to convey my thoughts more clearly in written, verbal, & visual formats. One of the best tips I’ve picked up is to forget what I learned about writing in high school. There we were taught to start with an intro -> explanation -> conclusion. In reality most stakeholders want it the other way around, start with the most important point(s) you want people to takeaway and then dive into the supporting content.

My Limitations

I can easily get sucked down rabbit holes if I come across something that is interesting to me. Recently I was working with building a PDF form that had fields that could be filled programatically - essentially the customer would fill out a form via a bunch of input fields in our software and we would spit out a PDF with the info they entered. As I was building the PDF form I learnt that PDFs actually support some basic javascript and tried to create a form that would automatically sum some of the information so that users didn’t need to manually enter it. I spent hours trying different scripts and troubleshooting why it wasn’t working, only to find out that it won’t execute unless it’s opened in specific software like Adobe Acrobat. This was time spent on something minor that wouldn’t have made that much of an impact that I could have spent on more productive things that were actually relevant to my job.

Kind of related to the above, I also tend to be quite independent. I struggle to ask for help when I get stuck and want to find solutions on my own. Often this isn’t too harmful and just results in me spending a bit more time on something than necessary, but I know in this course I’m going to come across things that I can’t figure out on my own and will need to be mindful of time boxing myself and asking for help when I hit those walls.

When things get tough, if possible I will take a break. Step away from my computer and take some time to clear my head. If necessary I will reach out to my facilitator for help.

How and where I will seek help

I'll start by timeboxing myself to find a solution on my own.
If I'm unable to do so in a reasonable timeframe, or if the problem is time sensitive I'll ask in the foundations-help-desk channel, with a thorough explanation of the issue and screenshots/code snippets if relevant.
If I'm unable to find a resolution there, then I will reach out to my facilitator directly.

Dealing with conflict

Being a Product Manager there were several times where while working with management on the direction of my team I disagreed with them about a particular solution or direction. I can’t share the specifics of what we were debating, but there was one case where they wanted to skip doing a piece of work so we could move onto other priorities but I felt that this work was important and we should not skip it.

In this case I wrote a summary of why I believed we needed to do it and organised a meeting for me to run through my thinking and discuss possible outcomes. After the meeting I had managed to convince them that the work was necessary and our team was able to proceed. So it was kind of a happy ending, but how could I have handled this better?

I think the sentiment of “Share early, share often” could have helped to prevent the disagreement from starting in the first place. If I had my thoughts and evidence prepared and visible earlier, the others would have had a better understanding of where I was coming from and why I felt strongly that this was important.
